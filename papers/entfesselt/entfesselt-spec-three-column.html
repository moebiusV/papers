When Johann Christoph Gottsched (1700–1766, Leipzig professor of rhetoric who modeled German prose on Ciceronian Latin; his rules imposed the verb-final subordinate clause and suppressed do-support) and Johann Christoph Adelung (1732–1806, lexicographer whose dictionary and grammar codified High German as a Latinate prestige register, systematically excluding dialectal and spoken features) and their successors set about standardizing German in the 18th century, their explicit models were Latin grammar and French prose style (French being the contemporary prestige language, itself heavily Latinized). They valued:
  • Periodic sentence structure with the verb withheld until the end of the clause, modeled on Ciceronian Latin, where the verb conventionally appears last. This became the German subordinate-clause verb-final rule.
  • Synthetic case morphology over analytic prepositional constructions, because Latin expresses grammatical relations through case endings rather than prepositions. This suppressed the natural German drift toward von-constructions.
  • Single-negation logic, because Latin grammarians taught that two negatives make a positive. This killed the native Germanic double negation that Middle High German (MHG, c. 1050–1350) used freely.
  • Complex nominal compounds written as single orthographic units, possibly influenced by Latin's own tendency toward long compound adjectives and the scholarly habit of creating technical terminology through compounding rather than periphrasis.
  • Suppression of do-support (tun-periphrasis), because Latin has no equivalent construction, and the prescriptivists considered any structure without a Latin parallel to be "vulgar."

English u/v and i/j: positional orthographic conditioning. Until around 1630, English writing used u and v as positional variants of the same letter: v word-initially, u within the word: vnder, vp, vpon, haue, loue, neuer, seuen. The letter represented both the vowel /u/ and the consonant /v/; position in the word determined which spelling appeared, not which sound was intended. Similarly, i and j were positional variants: j appeared initially before vowels (ioy, iudge), i elsewhere. Both distinctions were purely graphic conventions with no phonemic basis. This is exactly parallel to the Swedish þ→t/d split: one underlying form, two surface realizations depending on position. The principle appears in orthography as readily as in phonology. The conditioning factor (PIE accent position) is no longer present in any living language, but the result is fossilized in the paradigms. Danish "soft d": conditioned by post-vocalic position, phonetically identical to English "th". Swedish and Norwegian do not have this rule; their d is a true stop throughout. This makes Danish, of the three mainland Scandinavian languages, phonetically the closest to English on this particular feature: both languages have the voiced dental fricative/approximant [ð]; they simply arrived at it by different historical routes and write it with different letters. On noun case restoration. Case is a harder question than gender. English lost case from nouns entirely by the 14th century; only the pronouns kept it. Restoring case marking to nouns would require choosing declension classes, deciding which of the four cases to restore, and the result would read as constructed rather than archaic. The preferable middle ground: the genitive apostrophe-s (the king's crown) is the Germanic genitive already fully alive in English, and it should be used systematically over the Norman French of construction wherever the underlying relationship is possessive. The oblique pronouns (me, him, her, us, them, whom) are already there and should be used consistently. The Re-centering amplifies what survived; it does not reconstruct what was lost.
