  A reader encountering this project for the first time may worry that it is anglicizing German, that revealing the similarity to English amounts to erasing something distinctively German. This concern deserves a precise answer: every feature Entfesselt restores is attested in medieval German, in the living southern dialects, in the spoken vernacular that has never obeyed the written standard. Nothing is imported from English. The reverse is true. The conventions that make German look alien to its siblings were imposed on the written language in the 18th century, in the name of Latin and French prestige. Entfesselt removes those impositions. The result resembles English because English was never subjected to them; both languages kept the same original Germanic character, and uncovering German\'s reveals it. ### The High German Consonant Shift and the North/South Divide / Die hochdeutsche Lautverschiebung und die Nord-Süd-Spaltung

  English                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  Entfesselt                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         Standard German

  The single most important phonological event in Germanic history is the High German Consonant Shift (the Second Sound Shift), which occurred roughly 500--700 CE in the Old High German period. It affected only the southern, inland Germanic dialects (what became Standard German) and left the coastal/northern dialects untouched. The shift: voiceless stops became fricatives or affricates in specific environments: *p* → *pf/ff*, *t* → *ts/ss*, *k* → *ch/ck*, *d* → *t* (in some positions). Result: English *ship* / German *Schiff*; English *water* / German *Wasser*; English *make* / German *machen*; English *eat* / German *essen*; English *that* / German *das*.   Das einzige wichtigste phonologische Ereignis in der germanischen Geschichte ist die Hoch Deutsche Konsonanten Verschiebung (die Zweite Laut Verschiebung), die ungefähr 500--700 n. Chr. in der Alt Hoch Deutschen Periode aufgetreten ist. Die hat nur die südlichen, binnenländischen germanischen Dialekte betroffen (was Standard Deutsch geworden ist) und hat die küstennahen/nördlichen Dialekte unberührt gelassen. Die Verschiebung: stimmlose Stops sind in spezifischen Umgebungen zu Frikativen oder Affrikaten geworden: *p* → *pf/ff*, *t* → *ts/ss*, *k* → *ch/ck*, *d* → *t* (in manchen Positionen). Ergebnis: Englisch *ship* / Deutsch *Schiff*; Englisch *water* / Deutsch *Wasser*; Englisch *make* / Deutsch *machen*; Englisch *eat* / Deutsch *essen*; Englisch *that* / Deutsch *das*.   Das bedeutendste phonologische Ereignis der germanischen Geschichte ist die hochdeutsche Lautverschiebung (die zweite Lautverschiebung), die ungefähr zwischen 500 und 700 n. Chr. in der althochdeutschen Periode stattfand. Sie betraf nur die südlichen, binnenländischen germanischen Dialekte (das spätere Standarddeutsch) und ließ die Küsten- und Norddialekte unberührt. Die Verschiebung: stimmlose Verschlusslaute wurden in bestimmten Umgebungen zu Frikativen oder Affrikaten: *p* → *pf/ff*, *t* → *ts/ss*, *k* → *ch/ck*, *d* → *t* (in manchen Positionen). Ergebnis: Englisch *ship* / Deutsch *Schiff*; Englisch *water* / Deutsch *Wasser*; Englisch *make* / Deutsch *machen*; Englisch *eat* / Deutsch *essen*; Englisch *that* / Deutsch *das*.
  The languages that did NOT shift (Old English, Frisian, Dutch, Plautdietsch, the Scandinavian languages) stayed on the same consonant side as English. This is why English and Dutch feel consonantally similar and Standard German feels distant. It is not deep divergence; it is one specific historical event, about 1,400 years ago, confined to one dialect region.   Germanic sound correspondences are learnable, not chaotic, because shifts almost never apply uniformly to a phoneme regardless of context. | **On noun case restoration.** Case is a harder question than gender. English lost case from nouns entirely by the 14th century; only the pronouns kept it. Restoring case marking to nouns would require choosing declension classes, deciding which of the four cases to restore, and the result would read as constructed rather than archaic.   The Entfesselt Wörterbuch is the noun-unstacking dictionary: a community-maintained lookup table where native speakers and linguists contribute and vote on how compounds should be split. This is a separate project from the centroid dictionary (a long-term research project combining vocabulary across all Germanic languages; TBD). The Wörterbuch records operational decisions: which compounds unfuse and how. Every encountered compound is logged to `compounds-for-curation.tsv` and reviewed against three bins: **Bin A** (NEVER unfuse; opaque: Handschuh, Schmetterling). **Bin B** (OPTIONALLY unfuse; transparent, benefiting from splitting). **Bin C** (LEAVE ALONE; short, lexicalized, no benefit). Community curation is not required for operation --- the LLM handles all decisions from day one --- but once a compound is ratified in the Wörterbuch, it overrides the LLM and ensures cross-document consistency. Native speaker input is the authority on which compounds feel natural split and which feel wrong; the LLM proposes, the community confirms.
