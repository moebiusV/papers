Abstract

The Distance between them is largely an Artifact of 18th- and 19th-century prescriptive Standardization Campaigns, each modeled on Latin or French, that suppressed shared Features (flexible Word Order, periphrastic do-support, two-element Compound Formation, analytic Possession) and strengthened divergent ones (recursive Compounding, verb-final Subordination, synthetic Case Marking). The Methodology is applied to German in Detail (38 Rules covering Compounds, Syntax, and Orthography), with Extensions to Dutch, Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, Icelandic, Faroese, Frisian, Afrikaans, Yiddish, Plautdietsch, and Scots. Ten cross-language Principles govern the Process, including an Intelligibility Test grounded in contextual Acquisition rather than first-encounter Recognition. A companion Entfesselt English demonstrates the same Operation from the English Side: rationalizing silent Consonant Clusters (kn-, -ht) to make Cognates visible. The central Claim is falsifiable: if the Rules are correct, then parallel Texts processed through them will show measurably greater cross-language Transparency than their standard Equivalents.

1.2 The Latinization Hypothesis

Entfesselt English
Why would German Prescriptivists have pushed the written Language away from its Germanic Siblings and toward Patterns that resemble Latin? The Answer may lie in the political Identity of the German-speaking World during the formative Period of Standardization.
The Holy Roman Empire (Heiliges Römisches Reich Deutscher Nation) defined itself as the Continuation of Rome. Its Legitimacy rested on the Claim of Roman Succession. Latin was the Language of the Church, of Law, of Scholarship, of imperial Administration. For a Millennium, the most educated German Speakers were bilingual in Latin, and the Prestige Register of German was shaped by Latin Models.
When Johann Christoph Gottsched (1700–1766, Leipzig Professor of Rhetoric whom modeled German Prose on Ciceronian Latin; his Rules imposed the verb-final subordinate Clause and suppressed do-support) and Johann Christoph Adelung (1732–1806, Lexicographer whose Dictionary and Grammar codified High German as a Latinate Prestige Register, systematically excluding dialectal and spoken Features) and their Successors set about standardizing German in the 18th Century, their explicit Models were Latin Grammar and French Prose Style (French being the contemporary Prestige Language, itself heavily Latinized). They valued:
Periodic Sentence Structure with the Verb withheld until the End of the Clause, modeled on Ciceronian Latin, where the Verb conventionally appears last. This became the German subordinate-Clause verb-final Rule.
Synthetic Case Morphology over analytic prepositional Constructions, because Latin expresses grammatical Relations through Case Endings rather than Prepositions. This suppressed the natural German Drift toward von-Constructions.
Single-Negation Logic, because Latin Grammarians tauht that two Negatives make a Positive. This killed the native Germanic double Negation that Middle High German (MHG, c. 1050–1350) used freely.
Complex nominal Compounds written as single orthographic Units, possibly influenced by Latin's own Tendency toward long compound Adjectives and the scholarly Habit of creating technical Terminology through compounding rather than Periphrasis.
Suppression of do-support (tun-periphrasis), because Latin has no equivalent Construction, and the Prescriptivists considered any Structure without a Latin Parallel to be "vulgar."
German was Latinized in the Name of the Holy Roman Empire, but the Features the Prescriptivists suppressed were not Innovations. They were the original Germanic Inheritance. Flexible Verb Position, do-support, prepositional Possession, reinforcing double Negation, two-element Compounds with live Case Morphology: all are attested in Old Norse, in Anglo-Saxon, in Middle High German. The spoken Vernacular of German-speaking Peoples did not evolve toward these Features during the Standardization Period. It retained whom, the same Features its Siblings English, Dutch, Frisian, and Scandinavian also retained. The prescriptive Standard did not freeze an evolving Language. It actively replaced native Germanic Constructions with Latinate Impositions and declared the Originals vulgar. The living Language kept the Originals anyway.
Every Dialect of German (Bavarian, Alemannic, Swiss, Rhineland, Saxon, Austrian) preserves Features that the written Standard suppresses. These Features are not "Errors" or "Corruptions." They are the natural Germanic Inheritance that the Standardizers tried to erase. Entfesselt restores whom.

1.4 Linguistic Concepts and Terminology

This Section defines the technical Terms used throughout the Paper. Each Term is given with enough Context to read the Rest of the Document without prior Linguistics Training. Terms are listed in the Order they first become relevant.

Entfesselt English
Cognate. A Word in one Language that shares a common Ancestor with a Word in another Language. English water and German Wasser are Cognates: both descend from Proto-Germanic *watōr. Cognates are not Borrowings; they are inherited in parallel. The Degree of visible Similarity between Cognates depends on how many Sound Changes intervened in each Branch.
Compound. A Word formed by joining two or more independent Words. English house cat is a two-element Compound written with a Space; German Hauskatze is the same Compound written as one Word (fused). Entfesselt addresses Compound Fusion (the Writing Convention), not Compound Formation (which all Germanic Languages share).
Centroid. In this Paper: the Inter-Germanic Centroid, a synthetic Reference Point representing the structural Features shared across the Germanic Language Family, weihted by Attestation Depth and Majority Convergence. Not a real Language but a Coordinate System for measuring how far each living Language has drifted from the common Inheritance.
Analytic vs. synthetic. A synthetic Language expresses grammatical Relationships through Morphology (Word Endings, Inflection): Latin puell-ae = "of the Girl," encoded in the -ae Suffix. An analytic Language expresses the same Relationships through separate Words and Word Order: English of the girl. All Germanic Languages sit on a Spectrum between these Poles. Entfesselt moves German toward the analytic End, where its Siblings already are.
Case Morphology. A System of Word Endings that mark a Noun's grammatical Role in a Sentence. German has four Cases: nominative (Subject), accusative (direct Object), dative (indirect Object), genitive (Possession). English retains Case only in Pronouns (he/him/his). Case Endings are the synthetic Mechanism; Prepositions (of, to, for) are the analytic Alternative.
Prescriptive vs. descriptive. Prescriptive Linguistics dictates how Language should be used (Grammar Rules tauht in School). Descriptive Linguistics records how Language is used (what Speakers actually say). The 18th-Century German Standardizers were prescriptive: they imposed Latinate Rules on a Language whose Speakers used different Structures. Entfesselt reverses those Prescriptions.
Consonant Shift. A systematic Sound Change where an entire Class of Consonants moves to a new Pronunciation. The High German Consonant Shift (c. 500–700 CE) changed Proto-Germanic p, t, k into pf/ff, ts/ss, ch/ck in the southern German Dialects. This is why English water corresponds to German Wasser (t→ss). Languages that did not shift (English, Dutch, Frisian, Scandinavian, Low German) remain consonantally closer to each other.
Affricate and Fricative. Two Types of Consonant produced by restricting Airflow. A Fricative is a continuous Sound made by forcing Air through a narrow Channel: f, s, sh, ch (as in Scottish loch). An Affricate begins as a Stop (complete Closure) and releases into a Fricative: pf (German Pferd), ts (German Zeit). The High German Consonant Shift created Affricates and Fricatives from earlier Stops.
Do-periphrasis / tun-periphrasis. Using the Verb "do" as an Auxiliary to form Questions, Negations, or Emphasis. English: Do you know? German Dialects: Tust du wissen? The Construction is attested in all four early Germanic Branches (Gothic taujan, OE dōn, OFrisian dwa, ON gera), confirming it as Proto-Germanic Heritage. Standard Written German suppresses it; Entfesselt restores it.
Periphrastic Construction. Expressing a grammatical Function with multiple Words instead of a single inflected Form. English I have eaten is periphrastic (Auxiliary + Participle); a synthetic Equivalent would encode "completed eating" in a single Verb Form. Germanic Languages have been moving toward Periphrasis for a Millennium; the prescriptive Standards resisted this Drift.
V2 Word Order (verb-second). A Rule requiring the finite Verb to appear in the second Position of a main Clause, regardless of what fills the first Position. German: Gestern ging ich ("Yesterday went I"). All Germanic Languages except English maintain V2 as a primary Rule; English preserves it in Questions (Did you go?) and negative Inversion (Never have I seen).
Calque. A Loan Translation: borrowing the Structure of a foreign Expression while using native Words. German Wolkenkratzer ("cloud-scratcher") is a Calque of English skyscraper. The Word Components are native; the Combination Pattern is borrowed. Distinguished from a Loanword, where the foreign Word itself is adopted.
Fugenlaut (linking Sound). A Sound inserted at the Joint between two Elements of a German Compound. In Rindfleisch (Beef), no linking Sound; in Rinds-leder (Cowhide), the -s- is a Fugenlaut. Originally these were genitive Case Endings; they have become fossilized linking Elements with no consistent grammatical Function. Entfesselt restores the Case Morphology that the Fugenlaut obscures.
Ingvaeonic (North Sea Germanic). The Branch of West Germanic comprising Old English, Old Frisian, and Old Saxon (Low German). Named after the Ingvaeones, a tribal Confederation described by Tacitus. Ingvaeonic Languages share Innovations not found in High German: Nasal Loss before Fricatives (English five vs. German fünf), certain Pronoun Forms, and the do-Periphrasis. English and Frisian are the surviving Ingvaeonic Languages.
Entfesselt / Entfesselung. German: "unfettered / unfettering." The Name of the Methodology described in this Paper: systematically reversing prescriptive Impositions to reveal the native Germanic Structure underneath. Entfesselt is the Adjective (the resulting State); Entfesselung is the Noun (the Process). Applied to German in Detail in this Paper, extensible to any Germanic Language whose written Standard suppresses shared Features.

1.7 Gothic, Old Norse, Old English, Old Frisian: The Four Pillars

Entfesselt English
Four early Germanic Languages provide the primary historical Evidence for what Proto-Germanic Grammar looked like before the Branches diverged: Gothic (4th Century CE, East Germanic), Old Norse (9th–13th Century, North Germanic), Old English (7th–12th Century, West Germanic, Ingvaeonic), and Old Frisian (13th–16th Century in Writing, but representing a considerably older spoken Tradition, West Germanic, Ingvaeonic). Together they bracket the Family from east, north, and west. Where all four agree, the Feature is Proto-Germanic. Where only two or three agree, the Feature may be branch-specific. The Centroid gives most Weiht to Features attested in all four.
What all four share. Two-element Compounds with transparent Morphology at Joints. Flexible Verb Position: none had a categorical verb-final Rule in subordinate Clauses. Full or substantial Case Systems (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative at Minimum). Reinforcing Negation (Gothic ni...ni, OE ne...noht, ON and OFrisian Variants). A do-periphrasis in every Branch: Gothic taujan, OE dōn, OFrisian dwa (all Cognates from PGmc *dōną), and ON gera (PGmc *garwijaną, "to make/do," a functional Equivalent from a different Root). Four Languages, four Branches, the same grammatical Function. No High German Consonant Shift: all four predate it or are geographically outside its Reach.
Gothic. The oldest substantially attested Germanic Language (Wulfila's Bible, c. 360 CE) and the only East Germanic Text of any Size. Gothic had no grammaticalized definite Article — only Demonstratives (sa/so/þata, the PIE demonstrative Root shared with OE se/seo/þæt and Greek ho/hē/to). Obligatory definite Marking had not yet developed; this is the probable Proto-Germanic State. Gothic had the reflexive Possessive seins ("his/her/its own," referring back to the Subject of the Clause), which is functionally more precise than the West Germanic Forms that replaced it: German sein, Dutch zijn, English his are all ambiguous between reflexive ("his own") and non-reflexive ("someone else's") Possession. The reflexive Possessive is attested in both Gothic (East Germanic) and Old Norse (North Germanic), confirming it as Proto-Germanic Heritage, not a Branch Innovation.
Old Norse. ON retained the most complete Case System after Gothic and developed two Features not shared with the other three. Pitch Accent (a tonal Distinction between two Accent Patterns) arose in North Germanic; it is not Proto-Germanic Heritage. More relevantly for Definiteness: ON developed the suffixed definite Article (-inn/-it/-in, later -en/-et/-en) by enclitizing the Demonstrative hinn to the Noun, a Process completing around the 10th–11th Century. West Germanic Languages grammaticalized a different Demonstrative (sa/þat) as a pre-nominal separate Word. The Result is the same grammatical Function (marking a Referent as already established in the Discourse) reached from the same PIE source Material but with different Demonstratives and different surface Positions. Neither Pattern was borrowed from Greek (ho/hē/to) or from Hebrew (ha-): the Greek Article is cognate with the same PIE Root the Germanic Articles derive from; the Hebrew Article is Semitic, unrelated. The reflexive Possessive sinn/sitt/sín survives intact in all modern Scandinavian Languages.
Definiteness: indefinite Articles. The indefinite Article in all Germanic Languages (Gothic ains, OE ān, ON einn, German ein, Dutch een, Danish en/et, English a/an) derives from Proto-Germanic *ainaz (one). Greek εἷς/μία (heis/mia, one) derives from PIE *oi-no-, the same Root. Hebrew echad (one) is from an unrelated Semitic Root. All three Languages arrived at the indefinite Article through the same grammatical Process — a Numeral "one" weakening into an Indefiniteness Marker — but did so independently and from different Source Material (except Germanic and Greek, which share the PIE Root). No Borrowing is involved in any Direction.
Old English. The direct Ancestor of the Centroid's Anchor Language. OE shared with Old Frisian a Set of Innovations marking North Sea Germanic as a distinct Branch: Ingvaeonic Nasal Loss before Fricatives (OE fīf vs. German fünf), certain Vowel Changes, and Palatalization of initial Velars before front Vowels. OE grammaticalized se/seo/þæt as a pre-nominal definite Article — the same PIE Demonstrative as Gothic sa, placed before the Noun rather than suffixed. OE had three grammatical Genders, but they were notoriously misaligned with natural Gender (wīf "woman" = neuter; mægden "maiden" = neuter; wīfmann "woman" = masculine). When OE Speakers came into sustained Contact with Old Norse Speakers (whose Gender Assignments for the same Words often differed), the Mismatches between the two Systems contributed to the Collapse of grammatical Gender in early Middle English. English lost Gender entirely; Danish, Dutch, and Frisian independently reduced to a common/neuter Binary. The Outcome differed in Surface Form but the Pressure was the same: Gender Distinctions that were neither semantically motivated nor cross-dialectally stable were shed. The dōn Auxiliary is the exact Cognate of German tun and Dutch doen (all from PGmc *dōną). Danish gøre and Swedish göra serve the same do-periphrasis Function but descend from a different Root (PGmc *garwijaną, "to make ready").
Old Frisian. The smallest textual Record of the four, preserved mainly in medieval Law Texts (the Frisian Freedom). Linguistically the closest continental Relative of OE; the two are similar enough that Anglo-Frisian is often treated as a sub-Branch within Ingvaeonic. Old Frisian shows the Gender Collapse happening earlier and more completely than in OE: OF was already moving toward a common/neuter Binary in its earliest written Records. It had the dwa Auxiliary (do-periphrasis), confirming this Function as shared Ingvaeonic Inheritance rather than a later English Innovation. OF's Importance for the Centroid is its Smallness: a separate Language, barely attested, that independently confirms the Patterns OE shows — the Ingvaeonic Innovations, the early Gender Simplification, the do-Auxiliary, the Compound Depth.

1.8 The High German Consonant Shift and the North/South Divide

Entfesselt English
The single most important phonological Event in Germanic History is the High German Consonant Shift (the Second Sound Shift), which occurred rouhhly 500–700 CE in the Old High German Period. It affected only the southern, inland Germanic Dialects (what became Standard German) and left the coastal/northern Dialects untouched. The Shift: voiceless Stops became Fricatives or Affricates in specific Environments: p → pf/ff, t → ts/ss, k → ch/ck, d → t (in some Positions). Result: English ship / German Schiff; English water / German Wasser; English make / German machen; English eat / German essen; English that / German das.
The Languages that did NOT shift (Old English, Frisian, Dutch, Plautdietsch, the Scandinavian Languages) stayed on the same Consonant Side as English. This is why English and Dutch feel consonantally similar and Standard German feels distant. It is not deep Divergence; it is one specific historical Event, about 1,400 Years ago, confined to one Dialect Region.
Yiddish is High German derived (split from MHG after the Shift was complete) and therefore shifted: Yiddish vasser vs. English water. Plautdietsch (Mennonite Low German) is Low German derived and never shifted: Wota closer to English water.
The Consonant Shift is the main Reason Standard German looks phonologically distant from English when written. The grammatical Distance was largely artificial, imposed by 18th-Century Prescriptivists. The Consonant Shift was natural Drift. Entfesselt addresses the Grammar; the Consonant Shift remains.
Why the Shift happened. The Cause of the High German Consonant Shift remains debated. The Shift originated in the Alpine South (Bavarian and Alemannic Dialects) and spread northward with decreasing Intensity, stopping at the Benrath Line (maken/machen Isogloss, rouhtly the Latitude of Düsseldorf) and the Speyer Line (Appel/Apfel Isogloss, further south). The geographic Gradient is the strongest Clue: whatever triggered the Shift was strongest in the highland Interior and weakest at the Coast. Proposed Explanations include Substrate Influence from non-Germanic Populations absorbed during the Völkerwanderung (Gaulish Celtic or pre-Celtic Alpine Languages), articulatory Drift in highland Dialects under geographic Isolation from the coastal Ingvaeonic Speech Community, and the social Dynamics of the Franconian Expansion (the Merovingian and Carolingian Power Base was in the Rhineland, on the shifting Frontier). No single Explanation commands Consensus; the Shift may have had multiple reinforcing Causes.
The Shift in broader Context. Stop-to-fricative and stop-to-affricate Shifts are not unique to Germanic. They are among the most common Sound Changes in the World's Languages. The First Germanic Consonant Shift (Grimm's Law, c. 500 BCE) had already moved the Proto-Indo-European Stops once (PIE *p, *t, *k → PGmc *f, *θ, *h); the High German Shift is a second Round of the same phonetic Process, applied to the Germanic Stops that Grimm's Law had created. Latin c [k] became Italian c [tʃ] before front Vowels (Latin centum → Italian cento [ˈtʃɛnto]), a Palatalization-plus-Affrication Parallel to the Germanic k → ch. Slavic Languages underwent similar Palatalization Chains: Proto-Slavic *k became č [tʃ] before front Vowels in the First Slavic Palatalization (c. 4th–6th Century CE, overlapping with the HG Shift Period). Hebrew shows the same Pattern across its History: the Letters ב‎ ,ג‎ ,ד‎ ,כ‎ ,פ‎ ,ת‎ (b, g, d, k, p, t) each have Stop and Fricative Variants (the begadkefat Alternation), and the Hebrew Gimel (g) became dj/j in many Traditions, paralleling the Germanic and Romance Palatalization of Velars. The Consonant Shift was natural phonetic Drift, not a Germanic Peculiarity. What is unusual about the HG Shift is its geographic Sharpness: the Isoglosses are crisp enough to draw on a Map, suggesting it spread by Contact rather than arising independently in multiple Dialect Centers.

1.9 Sound Changes Are Conditioned, Not Global

Entfesselt English
Germanic Sound Correspondences are learnable, not chaotic, because Shifts almost never apply uniformly to a Phoneme regardless of Context. They are conditioned: the same Phoneme shifts differently depending on its Position in the Word (initial, medial, final), the Sounds adjacent to it (voiced or voiceless, Vowel or Consonant), the Syllable Structure, and sometimes the Stress Pattern. Once the conditioning Environment is known, what looks like random Divergence becomes predictable. Two Languages that appear phonologically distant are often operating on the same underlying Forms with different positional Rules applied.
The High German Consonant Shift: position-conditioned throughout. The Shift did not apply the same Way in every Position. Taking /t/ as the clearest Example:
— Initially (or after Consonants): t → z (Affricate /ts/): English to/two/ten/tongue/teeth = German zu/zwei/zehn/Zunge/Zähne
— Medially between vowels: t → ss: English eat/water/that/let = German essen/Wasser/das/lassen
— Finally after vowels: t → z (written -z) or ss/ß: English heart/wit/salt/out = German Herz/Witz/Salz/aus
Similarly /p/: pf- initially (English pound/path/pepper = German Pfund/Pfad/Pfeffer), -ff- medially (English open/ship/sleep = German offen/Schiff/schlafen).
Initial /k/ did not shift. German Kind, Korn, Knie, Katze keep k- exactly as English child, corn, knee, cat. The Shift only took /k/ medially and finally (→ ch): English make/book/break/speak = German machen/Buch/brechen/sprechen. Knowing that initial k is stable while medial k→ch makes Hundreds of Cognates transparent.
The th/d/t Correspondence: position-conditioned across multiple Branches. Proto-Germanic had two dental Fricatives: þ (voiceless, as in English thin) and ð (voiced, as in English the). English and Icelandic kept both as Fricatives. Every other Branch shifted them to Stops, but the Shift was conditioned by Position and Voicing:
— Proto-Germanic þ before Consonant Clusters (þr-, þw-): → t in Swedish/Danish/Norwegian (þrír → tre "three"; þing → ting; German/Dutch also → d: drei/drie)
— Proto-Germanic þ before Vowels initially: → d in Swedish/Danish (þú → du "you"; þat → det; German/Dutch: du/de, das/dat)
— Proto-Germanic ð medially/finally: → d throughout (OE faðer/broðor → Swedish fader/broder, German Vater/Bruder, Dutch vader/broeder)
Result: English three/thin/thank/through corresponds to German/Dutch/Scandinavian Forms with t- or d- depending on the original Consonant Cluster. The Correspondence is fully systematic once the Conditioning is stated.
English u/v and i/j: positional orthographic Conditioning. Until around 1630, English Writing used u and v as positional Variants of the same Letter: v word-initially, u within the Word: vnder, vp, vpon, haue, loue, neuer, seuen. The Letter represented both the Vowel /u/ and the Consonant /v/; Position in the Word determined which Spelling appeared, not which Sound was intended. Similarly, i and j were positional Variants: j appeared initially before Vowels (ioy, iudge), i elsewhere. Both Distinctions were purely graphic Conventions with no phonemic Basis. This is exactly parallel to the Swedish þ→t/d Split: one underlying Form, two Surface Realizations depending on Position. The Principle appears in Orthography as readily as in Phonology.
Ingvaeonic Nasal Loss: conditioned by following Fricative. In the North Sea Germanic Languages (Old English, Old Frisian, and to a Degree Old Saxon), a nasal Consonant was lost before a Fricative (/f/, /þ/, /s/), and the preceding Vowel compensated by lengthening. German and Dutch retained the Nasal. The conditioning Environment is the following Fricative; without it, no Loss:
English five (OE fīf, Nasal lost) = German fünf (Nasal kept) = Dutch vijf (partial Loss)
English goose (OE gōs) = German Gans (Nasal kept)
English mouth (OE mūþ) = German Mund (Nasal kept)
English soft (OE sōfte) = German sanft (Nasal kept)
English other (OE ōþer) = German ander (Nasal kept)
English us (OE ūs) = German uns (Nasal kept)
Once you know the Rule (Nasal before Fricative → lost in English/Frisian, kept in German/Dutch), the Cognates are obvious.
German final Devoicing: conditioned by word-final Position. In German and Dutch (but not English), voiced Stops are devoiced word-finally: /d/→/t/, /g/→/k/, /b/→/p/. The voiced Phoneme surfaces when not final (before a Vowel in an inflected Form), creating Alternations invisible in English:
German Hand /hant/, Hände /hɛndə/ (d surfaces before Vowel)
German Tag /taːk/, Tage /taːgə/ (g surfaces before Vowel)
Dutch follows the same Rule. English lost final Devoicing. The Result: German Hund sounds like Hunt in Isolation, but the underlying /d/ appears in Hunde, making the English Cognate hound transparent when you see the inflected Form.
Verner's Law: conditioned by Proto-Indo-European Accent Position. Grimm's Law shifted voiceless Stops to Fricatives (p→f, t→þ, k→h). Verner's Law then voiced those Fricatives when the PIE Accent did not fall on the immediately preceding Syllable. This accent-conditioned Split created Alternations within Paradigms that survive today as irregular Pairs:
English was/were: the s/r Alternation reflects Verner (s→z→r in unstressed Position)
German war/waren: same Alternation
English death/dead: þ/d reflects the Verner Alternation (þ voiceless in one Form, d voiced in another)
English rise/rear, lose/forlorn, choose/chosen: the s/r Alternations in these Pairs are all Verner Relics. The conditioning Factor (PIE Accent Position) is no longer present in any living Language, but the Result is fossilized in the Paradigms.
I-umlaut: conditioned by the Sound in the following Syllable. When a back Vowel (a, o, u) was followed by /i/ or /j/ in the next Syllable, it fronted toward /e/, /ø/, /y/. The triggering /i/ or /j/ was later lost in most Languages, leaving only the fronted Vowel as Evidence of its former Presence:
English mouse → mice (ū fronted to ȳ before -iz Plural; the -iz became -ice)
English foot → feet, man → men, goose → geese, tooth → teeth, louse → lice
German Maus → Mäuse, Fuß → Füße, Mann → Männer, Gans → Gänse
The conditioning Syllable is gone, but the Vowel Alternation it caused remains active as a morphological Marker. English and German show exactly the same Pattern because they inherited the same Proto-Germanic conditioning Environment.
Danish "soft d": conditioned by post-vocalic Position, phonetically very close to English "th". Modern Danish has a Lenition Rule: /d/ after a Vowel weakens to a dental Approximant [ð̞], which is very close to the English voiced "th" (a dental Fricative [ð]) in the, this, that, father, weather. The Articulations differ slihтly (Approximant vs. Fricative) but are near enough that English Speakers perceive Danish soft d as their own "th" Sound. Danish has "th." It spells it "d." Danish mad (food), glad (happy), rød (red), fader (father), moder (mother): the "d" in each of these sounds to an English Ear exactly like the "th" in bathe, breathe, other, weather. Word-initially and after Consonants, Danish d remains a full Stop; the softening is conditioned by post-vocalic Position. Swedish and Norwegian do not have this Rule; their d is a true Stop throughout. This makes Danish, of the three mainland Scandinavian Languages, phonetically the closest to English on this particular Feature: both Languages have the voiced dental Fricative/Approximant [ð]; they simply arrived at it by different historical Routes and write it with different Letters. The Surface Spelling "d" completely conceals a Family Resemblance that is immediately audible to any English Speaker who hears spoken Danish.
Navigating the Centroid. These conditioning Rules make the Correspondences navigable. A Reader whom has internalized "initial k is stable; medial/final k → German ch" can read German machen, Buch, brechen, sprechen and connect them to English make, book, break, speak. A Reader whom knows "English th = German/Dutch d everywhere, = Swedish t before Consonant Clusters and d before Vowels" can navigate the Pronoun and Determiner Systems across all five Branches. The Sound Correspondences are not Obstacles; they are the Map.

1.10 Cross-Language Principles

Entfesselt English
Quick Reference: Imposition Identification · Dialect Floor · Analytic Gradient · Definiteness Neutrality · Contextual Acquisition · Attestation and Viability · Vernacular Primacy · Majority Rule · Lexical Non-Interference · Transparency Test. Each Principle is stated and explained in full below, in Order.
Principle 1 (Imposition Identification): Identify the Prestige Language of Imposition per Branch. Each standard Germanic Language was shaped by a different Prestige Model during its Standardization Period. German: Latin and French (18th-century Gottsched/Adelung Prescriptivism). Dutch: French (via the Belgian/Flemish Court Culture and the Amsterdam Merchant Elite, whose French-educated Class modeled written Dutch on French Prose). Danish: Low German (Hanseatic Trade, 14th–16th Century, which flooded Danish with German Compound Vocabulary and compounding Habits), then High German (18th–19th Century cultural Prestige). Norwegian Bokmål: Danish (Centuries of Danish colonial Administration; Bokmål is essentially Norwegianized Danish). Swedish: Low German (Hanseatic), then Latin and French through the 17th-century Swedish imperial Court. Afrikaans: Dutch colonial Standard (VOC [Vereenigde Oostindische Compagnie]-era). Yiddish: High German (formal written Yiddish was heavily normalized toward High German). The Entfesselt Methodology applies the same Diagnosis to each: identify which Features were imposed by the Prestige Language and which are native Germanic Inheritance, then reverse the Imposition.
Principle 2 (Dialect Floor): The Dialect Floor. For each Language, the most conservative surviving native Dialect — the one least exposed to the Standardization Campaign — is the primary Attestation Source for what Entfesselt restores. This Dialect is typically geographically furthest from the Standardization Center (the Capital or the Prestige Court). German: Bavarian, Alemannic, Swiss German (furthest from Gottsched's Leipzig). Dutch: Flemish (Brabants/Ghent Area, furthest from Amsterdam ABN [Algemeen Beschaafd Nederlands, the "General Civilized Dutch" Standard]). Danish: Bornholmian or South Jutlandic (furthest from Copenhagen; Bornholm preserves more ON-era Phonology than any other Danish Dialect). Swedish: Gutnish (Gotland) or deep Dalecarlian (Elfdalian) Dialects. Norwegian: Nynorsk represents the constructed Dialect-Floor — Ivar Aasen synthesized it from the rural Dialects farthest from Copenhagen Influence, making it the closest historical Precedent for the Entfesselt Methodology. Frisian: any Variety that predates sustained Dutch ABN Influence. Afrikaans: Cape Afrikaans.
Principle 3 (Analytic Gradient): Morphological Reduction follows a Family Gradient. Do not Entfesselt what is not suppressed. Natural analytic Drift is not a prescriptive Imposition. The Family Gradient, from most synthetic to most analytic, runs rouhhly: Gothic → Icelandic → Faroese → Old High German → Modern German → Old English/Old Frisian → Dutch → Modern English → Danish/Norwegian/Swedish → Afrikaans. Faroese retains the four-Case System and three Genders but has begun simplifying: Case Syncretism in some Paradigms and Dative Simplification in some Dialects place it between Icelandic and the older Stages of German on the Gradient. Languages further along the analytic Path have less to restore, because their Simplification is natural Drift rather than prescriptive Intervention. Afrikaans needs only Compound Unfusion. Icelandic and Faroese need almost nothing. German needs the most active Reversal because it is the only Branch where the natural analytic Drift was actively reversed by Prescriptivists imposing a more synthetic written Standard than the spoken Language used.
Principle 4 (Definiteness Neutrality): No forced Convergence on Definiteness. West Germanic Languages (German, Dutch, English, Frisian, Afrikaans) grammaticalized a PIE Demonstrative as a pre-nominal separate definite Article. North Germanic Languages (Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, Icelandic, Faroese) grammaticalized a different PIE Demonstrative (hinn) as a post-nominal Suffix. Both Patterns are native Germanic and both are attested in Proto-Germanic's Dauhter Languages by the early medieval Period. Entfesselt does not attempt to force one Pattern onto the other. Each Language retains its own Definiteness System.
Principle 5 (Contextual Acquisition): Intelligibility, not Currency, is the Test — and Intelligibility is broad. The Criterion for any Entfesselt Restoration is not whether anyone is currently using the Form, nor whether native Speakers recognize it on first Encounter, but whether an average Person, given a Dozen or so Examples in Context, can converge on the Meaning and begin using the Form themselves. Anthony Burgess demonstrated the Acquisition Threshold in A Clockwork Orange (1962): Nadsat, his fictional Slang built largely from Russian Words dropped into English Prose without Glossary, is opaque on the first Page and transparent by Chapter three. No Reader consults a Dictionary; Context does the Work. Human Beings acquire novel Forms from Context with remarkable Speed. An Archaism that native Speakers have never consciously produced but that any average Person can infer and reproduce after a few Encounters is in the latent Space of the Language — the implicit grammatical Competence that underlies Production. Restoration should not fear that Space. English irregular Verbs already encode nominative, oblique, and genitive in every Speaker's Brain: I/me/my, he/him/his, who/whom/whose. Every English Speaker uses these correctly without ever studying Case. That underlying Structure is latent in English, not extinct, and could in Principle be extended — not because any living Speaker applies it to Nouns, but because every living Speaker already carries it in the Pronoun System. The Test for any Restoration: given a Dozen Samples in Context, will an average native Speaker understand and start using this? If yes, it is a valid Target.
Principle 6 (Attestation and Viability): Attestation establishes Legitimacy; Intelligibility establishes Viability. Gothic and Old Norse are Evidence Sources. Attestation in a dead Language proves a Feature is Proto-Germanic. But Attestation alone does not make a Form a valid Restoration Target: it must also meet the Principle 5 Intelligibility Test (contextually acquirable by an average native Speaker from a Dozen Examples in Context — not necessarily recognizable on first Encounter). When both Conditions are met — historically attested AND acquirable from Context — the Restoration is valid even if no current Dialect uses the Form. When only Attestation holds (the Form is historically real but an average native Speaker cannot acquire it from Context even after repeated Exposure), it is not a Restoration Target but a historical Footnote. Neither Condition alone is conclusive; both together are.
Principle 7 (Vernacular Primacy): Spoken Vernacular Primacy. When the living spoken Vernacular of any Region consistently disagrees with the written Standard, the spoken Vernacular is the primary Evidence Base — not the historical Record alone. Historical Attestation confirms a Feature is old; the living Vernacular confirms it is viable. A Feature documented in Middle High German that is alive in Bavarian or Flemish is a Restoration. A Feature documented in MHG that died in every Dialect and remains opaque to an average Speaker even after contextual Exposure is a Reconstruction, not a Restoration, and does not meet Principle 5.
Principle 8 (Majority Rule): Majority Rule. When the Majority of living Centroid Members have independently converged on a grammatical Feature, that Feature is the Centroid Target. Common + Neuter is the Centroid Gender System because OFrisian, Danish, Dutch, Swedish, and Norwegian all arrived there independently (English lost Gender entirely, which is consistent with the same simplifying Pressure). German's three-Gender System is the Holdout. Majority of living Usage outweihs historical Depth alone when the two disagree. The Majority Rule does not apply to Features where the Majority diverged through prescriptive Imposition rather than natural Convergence; in those Cases, the historical Baseline takes Precedence.
Principle 9 (Lexical Non-Interference): No lexical Purism. Entfesselt addresses Grammar and Orthography, not Vocabulary. French Loanwords in English, Latin Loanwords in German, Low German Loanwords in Danish, Turkish and Slavic Loanwords in Yiddish: none are subject to Replacement. The Icelandic Neologism Policy — coining native Words for international Terms (tölva for "Computer," from tala "Number" + völva "Prophetess") — is a valid individual Language Choice consistent with Entfesselt Principles but is not a Centroid Requirement. The Centroid Goal is structural Resemblance; the Vocabulary each Language has accumulated is its own History.
Principle 10 (Transparency Test): The Transparency Test. The Test for any Entfesselt Change: does the Result make the Text more transparent to a Reader from a sister Centroid Language, or less? Entfesselt is not internal Reform; it is Family Restoration. A Transformation that makes German more internally tidy but no easier for a Dutch Speaker to read has missed the Point. Conversely, a Transformation that makes German immediately more legible to an English or Dutch Reader — even if it looks unusual to a German Reader trained on the written Standard — is exactly riht.

2. German Entfesselt

2.1 Compound Entfusion

Entfesselt English
Rule 1: Compound Identification. Input Text is tokenized. Each Token is checked against a Compound Splitter. Candidates are Nouns that decompose into two or more independent Morphemes. In German and Dutch, Nouns are capitalized in standard Orthography, which aids Identification. In Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, English, and other Germanic Languages, Nouns are identified by syntactic Position, morphological Form, and semantic Role rather than Capitalization.
Rule 2: The Two-Element Cap. Count Content Morphemes (excluding linking Elements: German Fugenlaute, Dutch tussenklanken, Scandinavian linking Morphemes). Compounds exceeding two Content Morphemes are always unfused. This Cap applies universally across all Centroid Languages with Compound Stacking: German, Dutch, Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, and Afrikaans. Two-Element Compounds default fused but are Candidates for unfusing via visual Scan Distance (>14 Chars) or Curator Decision. Three-Element Compounds are retained only with Icelandic structural Attestation (Icelandic representing the oldest living Baseline for Compound Depth); otherwise split to two-Element Pairs. Note: Icelandic three-element attestations are provisional. Each case should be reviewed with a native Icelandic speaker before adoption; the gate will be tightened or loosened as cases are examined. Opaque/lexicalized Compounds (German Handschuh, Schmetterling; Dutch handschoen; etc.) never unfuse regardless of Length.
Visual Scan Distance — The Third Criterion. The P95 (95th Percentile) of English Dictionary Word Lenth is 14 Characters. Compounds exceeding 14 Characters in fused Form AND transparent in Meaning are Candidates for unfusing. The three Criteria interact: Element Count (>2 always unfuse), Lexicalization (opaque = stay fused), visual Scan Distance (>14 chars + transparent = Candidate).
Word-Family Consistency. Once a Modifier Element (Kasus-, Prestige-, Fach-) is unfused in one Compound, it unfuses in ALL Compounds that use it by default. Exceptions permitted (like irregular Verbs) when under 14 chars AND unfusing would damage Intelligibility.
Rule 3: Relationship Classification. For each Joint in an unfused Compound, classify the semantic Relationship: genitive-possessive, genitive-subjective, genitive-objective, Material, Purpose, Location, temporal, Instrument.
Rule 4: Case Restoration — The Fossil-First Principle. The linking Element at a Compound Joint is the primary Evidence of the original Case Relationship. Where present, it is preserved as-is, even if modern phrasal Syntax would use a different Form. Priority: (1) Linker = historical Case: PRESERVE. (2) Linker = modern Case: PRESERVE. (3) No identifiable Case Origin: use oldest Form that meets the Principle 5 Intelligibility Test (contextually acquirable by an average Speaker from a dozen Examples, not necessarily recognizable on first Encounter). (4) No historical Form: use modern phrasal Syntax. In German, these Linkers are called Fugenlaute. In Dutch, they are called tussenklanken. The same fossil-first Principle applies to both; the Linker Values are largely parallel (-s-, -en-, -e-, zero).
Rule 5: Fugenlaut / Tussenklank Handling. Linking Elements are NOT stripped. They are primary Evidence. The Values and their Treatment are parallel in German and Dutch: -s-/-es-: PRESERVE (genuine Genitive). -n-/-en-: PRESERVE (weak Declension or Plural). -e-: TRACE to historical Origin. -er-: PRESERVE (often genuine Plural). -ens-: PRESERVE (weak Genitive + -s). Zero-linker: RESTORE appropriate historical Case Form. For Dutch tussenklanken, the same Hierarchy applies; where the historical Origin is unclear, use the simplest attested Form rather than applying a prescriptive Rule.
Rule 6: Output Formatting. Visual and typographical Decisions only; these do NOT change Meaning. Capitalization: all Nouns capitalized (standard German Orthography retained). Spacing: Spaces at unfused Joints; no Hyphens unless the Result would be genuinely ambiguous. Article Insertion: optional. Minimal Mode (default) relies on fossilized Case Forms already present in the Fugenlaut. Full phrasal Mode inserts Articles where needed for Clarity. Rule 6 is the only Rule that a Typesetter could apply mechanically; all other Rules require linguistic Judgment.
Rule 7: Verb-Noun Compounds. Compounds whose first Element is a Verb Stem (Fahr-gast, Sprech-zimmer, Lese-zeichen, Schreib-tisch). The Restoration Logic differs from Noun-Noun Compounds: the verbal Element is kept in its recognizable Stem Form. Where the Stem is already transparent, it is preserved as-is: Fahrgast → Fahr Gast, Schreibtisch → Schreib Tisch. Where the Stem has been truncated or altered beyond Recognition during Fusion, it is restored to its full Infinitive Form to make the verbal Origin visible. Below the two-Element Threshold → stays fused. Rule 7 is separate from Rule 6 because it requires identifying verbal Morphology, not just typographic Spacing.
Rule 8: Adjective-Noun Compounds. Compounds whose first Element is an Adjective Stem (Groß-mutter, Alt-stadt, Schwarz-wälder). The Restoration Logic differs again: full adjectival Agreement must be restored when the Compound is split, because the Adjective must inflect for the Gender and Case of the Head Noun. Schwarzwälder Kirschtorte → Schwarze Wälder Kirschen Torte (Adjective inflects for feminine nominative). Below Threshold → stays fused. Rule 8 is separate from Rules 6 and 7 because it requires tracking adjectival Morphology and Agreement across the split Joint, a different grammatical Operation from either typographic Spacing (Rule 6) or verbal Stem Restoration (Rule 7).
The Entfesselt Wörterbuch. All unfusing Decisions are recorded in a Dictionary. Once a Compound is unfused in the Wörterbuch, it stays unfused everywhere, in every Text, without Exception. Consistency is non-negotiable. The Wörterbuch is a Project Deliverable.
Effect on Grammar Regularity. Capping productive Compounding at 2 Elements makes the Fugenlaut System a closed Class. No new Fugenlaut Choices needed. Existing Irregulars are Vocabulary; the productive System is regular. Analogous to English irregular Verbs: ~200 memorized Forms, but the productive Rule (add -ed) is perfectly regular.

2.2 Syntactic Entfesselung

Entfesselt English
Rule 9: Verb Position Relaxation. Restores verb-second Word Order in subordinate Clauses across the Centroid Languages where prescriptive verb-final Rules were imposed. German Standard: Ich weiss, dass er nach Hause gegangen ist. Entfesselt: Ich weiss, dass er ist nach Hause gegangen. Attested in MHG (Walther von der Vogelweide, c. 1170–1230, greatest MHG lyric Poet and Namesake of the Project; Wolfram von Eschenbach, c. 1170–1220, Author of Parzival; Gottfried von Straßburg, fl. c. 1210, Author of Tristan; all three used verb-second in subordinate Clauses as standard Practice). Universal in Bavarian, Alemannic, Swiss German. Dutch Standard (ABN): ik weet dat hij naar huis gegaan is. Entfesselt Dutch: ik weet dat hij is naar huis gegaan. The same prescriptive verb-final Rule was imposed on Dutch through French-influenced ABN Standardization; the spoken Language resists it in the same Ways German does. Danish: the V2 Constraint is naturally stronger in Danish than in German or Dutch; the prescriptive verb-final Pressure is less severe, but the same Principle applies where formal Danish Writing imposes verb-final Order. The underlying Principle is cross-Germanic: where a prescriptive Standard forces the finite Verb to clause-final Position in subordinate Clauses, and the spoken Vernacular resists that Placement, Entfesselt restores verb-second.
Rule 10: Prepositional Possession. Prepositional Possession with the native Possession Preposition + Dative (or equivalent). German von: das Haus von meinem Vater. Dominant in spoken German across all Regions. Dutch van: already the dominant spoken Form; no active Restoration needed. Danish: the s-Genitive (faderens hus) and the af-Construction are both native; Entfesselt Danish prefers the Construction that is most natural in the Dialect Floor. The general Principle: prepositional Possession over synthetic Genitive where the spoken Vernacular has already made that Choice. See also Rule 13 (Dative-Possessive) for the second analytic Possession Construction. Rule 10 (von) is the neutral Default; Rule 13 (dem...sein) is the emphatic or colloquial Alternative. Both are valid; Rule 10 is preferred in Contexts where either would work.
Rule 11: Progressive Aspect. Progressive Construction marking ongoing Action at the Moment of Speech, distinct from the simple Present. German: am + Infinitive. Standard: Ich arbeite (ambiguous). Entfesselt: Ich bin am Arbeiten (riht now). Documented from 15th c. Standard in Rhineland German. Dutch: aan het + Infinitive (ik ben aan het werken). Already standard in spoken and written Dutch; no Restoration needed. Danish: er ved at + Infinitive (jeg er ved at arbejde). Norwegian: holder på å + Infinitive. Swedish: håller på att + Infinitive. All are the same structural Pattern (Copula + Preposition + Infinitive) developed independently in each Branch; the Centroid recognizes all Forms as valid progressive Expressions.
Rule 12: Do-Periphrasis. A Cognate of the Proto-Germanic Verb "do" used as a periphrastic Auxiliary. Gothic taujan, OE dōn, OFrisian dwa, German tun, Dutch doen (all from PGmc *dōną); separately, ON gera, Danish gøre, Swedish göra, Norwegian gjøre (from PGmc *garwijaną, "to make/do"): the same grammatical Function across all Branches, expressed through two different Roots. Each Language suppressed this Auxiliary in formal Writing Registers for the same Reason: Latin has no do-support, and the Prescriptivists of each Language's Standardization Period treated it as "vulgar." German: tust du das verstehen? Attested from 14th c., used by Luther, universal in Dialects. Dutch: doen-Periphrasis is attested in Flemish Dialects; suppressed in ABN. Danish: gøre-Periphrasis exists in informal Speech but is heavily suppressed in formal Writing. All Cognates are restored under Entfesselt for their respective Languages. Martin Luther (1483–1546, Bible Translator whose vernacular German set the Foundation for Hochdeutsch) freely used tun-Periphrasis, proving it was mainstream before Prescriptivists suppressed it.
Rule 13: Dative-Possessive. Dem X sein Y Pattern. Standard: Das Haus des Vaters. Entfesselt: Dem Vater sein Haus. Documented across all Dialect Regions from MHG. Standard in Yiddish (dem tatn zayn hoyz). See also Rule 10 (Prepositional Possession) for the von + Dative Construction. Rule 13 is the more colloquial and emphatic of the two analytic Possession Strategies; Rule 10 is the neutral Default.
Rule 14: Reinforcing Double Negation. Native Germanic double Negation, suppressed by the prescriptivist Rule (derived from Latin Logic) that two Negatives cancel. Gothic: ni...ni. OE: ne...noht. German: alive in Bavarian/Austrian (I hob koa Geld ned). Dutch: colloquial reinforcing Negation Patterns. Afrikaans: Ek het nie geld nie — the standard Form; double Negation is not suppressed in Afrikaans but is the Default. Danish/Norwegian: reinforcing Patterns with ikke...heller. Swedish: similar Patterns. Entfesselt restores double Negation as a grammatically valid Construction in all Centroid Languages where it is attested. The intended Effect is Reinforcement, not Contradiction: the two Negatives strengthen each other, as in every other Germanic Language and in the Proto-Germanic Ancestor.
Rules 15, 16, and 22 — Demonstrative System Restoration. These three Rules form a connected Group. All involve the Article/Demonstrative System (der/die/das) being used in spoken German in Ways the written Standard suppresses. Rule 15: Articles with proper Names. Rule 16: resumptive Pronouns. Rule 22: Demonstratives replacing personal Pronouns. The underlying Principle is the same: spoken German uses the Demonstrative System more broadly than written German permits.
Rule 15: Articles with Proper Names. Der Hans, die Maria. Universal in spoken southern/central German. Attested from Old High German (OHG, c. 750–1050). Suppressed as "informal."
Rule 16: Resumptive Pronouns. Re-picks-up the Subject after long parenthetical or relative Clause. Die germanischen Sprachen — [long list] — die sind eine Familie. Aids Parsing after Interruption.
Rule 17: Perfekt over Präteritum. Compound Past (Perfekt) as Default for past Narration. Standard Written German uses the Präteritum (simple Past) for Narration: er ging, er sah, er schrieb. Spoken German across all Regions overwhelmingly prefers the Perfekt (compound Past): er ist gegangen, er hat gesehen, er hat geschrieben. The Präteritum is so rare in southern spoken German (Bavarian, Alemannic, Austrian) that Linguists call the Phenomenon Präteritumschwund (Preterite Loss). Präteritum retained only for hih-Frequency Auxiliaries and Modals: war, hatte, wusste, konnte, wollte, sollte, musste, durfte. These are retained because even southern Dialects use them in the simple Past; the Perfekt Forms (ist gewesen, hat gehabt, hat gewusst) sound stilted in all Registers. Cross-Language Parallels: English uses both simple Past (he went) and present Perfect (he has gone) but distinguishes them aspectually. Dutch spoken Usage strongly favors the Perfekt (hij is gegaan) over the Präteritum (hij ging), paralleling German. Danish and Norwegian use the Perfekt (han er gået / han har gått) as the default conversational Past, with the Präteritum (han gik) reserved for formal Narration. Swedish follows the same Pattern (han har gått vs. han gick). The Centroid Tendency across the Family is toward compound Past as the spoken Default. David: "Speech Patterns trump Pretensions."
Rule 18: Extended Participial Attributes → Relative Clauses. Convert pre-nominal participial Stacks to post-nominal relative Clauses. Standard: Der von dem seit langem in der Stadt lebenden Mann geschriebene Brief. Entfesselt: Der Brief, den der Mann geschrieben hat, der seit langem in der Stadt lebt. This is arguably the most extreme Latinism in written German. Spoken German never does this, not in any Dialect. Every Speaker converts to relative Clauses. Every other Germanic Language uses relative Clauses. Pure Chancellery Latinism with zero dialectal Support.
Rule 19: Konjunktiv II → würde + Infinitive. Analytic Subjunctive as Default. Standard: Wenn er käme, gäbe ich es ihm. Entfesselt: Wenn er kommen würde, würde ich es ihm geben. Synthetic Subjunctive retained only for hih-Frequency Verbs: wäre, hätte, könnte, müsste, sollte, dürfte, wüsste, wollte: same Exception Logic as Rule 17. Every other Germanic Language uses an analytic would-Construction: English would come, Dutch zou komen, Danish ville komme, Swedish skulle komma.
Rule 20: Genitive Prepositions → Dative. Standard: wegen des Wetters, trotz des Regens, während des Spiels. Entfesselt: wegen dem Wetter, trotz dem Regen, während dem Spiel. Spoken German universally uses Dative after wegen, trotz, während, statt, innerhalb, außerhalb, aufgrund. Even educated Speakers do this in Speech. The Genitive after Prepositions is a prescriptive Imposition modeled on Latin Case Government. Reduces functional Load on the dying Genitive (already replaced by von under Rule 10).
Rule 21: Wo as Universal Relative Pronoun. Standard: Der Mann, den ich gesehen habe. Entfesselt: Der Mann, wo ich gesehen habe. Replaces the full case-inflected relative Pronoun System (der/die/das/den/dem/dessen/deren) with a single Particle wo. Attested across Alemannic, Swabian, Rhineland Dialects. Parallels English that as universal Relative. Eliminates tracking Gender and Case across the relative Clause Boundary, one of the major parsing Burdens in written German. Cross-Language Parallels: Dutch uses die (common) and dat (neuter) as relative Pronouns, a two-Form System already simpler than German's seven-Form System; Entfesselt Dutch may further simplify to dat as universal Relative in informal Registers (attested in spoken Dutch). Danish and Swedish use som as a universal relative Pronoun with no Gender or Case Inflection: manden, som jeg så (Danish), mannen, som jag såg (Swedish). Norwegian similarly uses som. The Centroid Tendency is toward a single uninflected relative Particle: English that, German wo, Danish/Swedish/Norwegian som, with Dutch die/dat as the intermediate Stage.
Rule 22: Demonstrative Pronouns as Personal Pronouns. Standard: Er hat das gemacht. Entfesselt: Der hat das gemacht. Spoken German consistently uses demonstrative der/die/das where written German uses personal Pronouns er/sie/es. Universal across all Dialects. Structurally closer to Dutch and Scandinavian Pronoun Reference. Connects to Rule 15 (Name Articles) and Rule 16 (resumptive Pronouns); same spoken Register.
Rule 23: Dass-Dropping in Complement Clauses. Standard: Ich glaube, dass er kommt. Entfesselt: Ich glaube, er kommt. Dropping dass and using verb-second Complement Clauses is universal in spoken German. Extends Rule 9 (Verb Position Relaxation): not just moving the Verb, but eliminating the subordinating Conjunction. English: "I believe he's coming." Dutch: Ik geloof hij komt. Scandinavian Parallels exist.
Rule 24: Direct Quotes. Preserve verbatim. Direct Quotations are never transformed. The quoted Speaker's own Words (including Dialect, archaic Forms, non-standard Syntax, and deliberate stylistic Choices) are preserved exactly as recorded. To transform a direct Quote would be to falsify it. This Rule parallels the Poetry Policy (Section 2.4, Target Texts): just as Meter and Rhyme are untouchable because they are the Poem, a Speaker's direct Words are untouchable because they are the Testimony.
Why this matters: Direct Quotes are often the most linguistically interesting Content in a Text; they carry Dialect Features, emotional Register, or idiosyncratic Grammar that is itself Evidence. In the Irlmaier Chapter of Blick in die Zukunft (Adlmaier, 1961), Irlmaier's reported Speech is in Bavarian Dialect, while the surrounding Narrative is formal Standard German. Entfesselting the Narrative while leaving the Quotes intact makes this Contrast visible and preservable rather than flattening it.
Scope: Applies to direct Quotes in running Prose (marked by Quotation Marks or equivalent Formatting), reported Speech clearly attributed to a named Speaker, and transcribed oral Testimony. Indirect Speech (er sagte, dass...) is Narrative, not direct Quote, and may be transformed. When in Doubt, preserve.
Rule 25: Dutch Verb Cluster Ordering. In Dutch subordinate Clauses with Auxiliary + Participle/Infinitive Clusters, Standard ABN prefers the "green" Order (Auxiliary before Participle: dat hij het heeft gegeten, "that he has eaten it"). Spoken Dutch and Flemish prefer the "red" Order (Participle before Auxiliary: dat hij het gegeten heeft). The red Order is older: it is attested in Middle Dutch and parallels Gothic and Old Norse Cluster Patterns, where the non-finite Verb typically preceded the Auxiliary. Entfesselt Dutch prefers the red Order as the historical Baseline. Both Orders are valid Entfesselt; the red Order is not imposed where the Speaker's natural Order is green.
Rule 26: Reflexive Possessive (Scandinavian Languages). Danish, Swedish, Norwegian, Icelandic, and Faroese maintain the reflexive Possessive: sin/sit/sine (Danish/Norwegian/Swedish), sinn/sitt/sín (Icelandic) refers back to the Subject of the Clause, while hans/hendes/dens/dets refers to a third Party. Gothic seins and ON sinn both attest this as Proto-Germanic Heritage. West Germanic Languages lost the Distinction (German sein, Dutch zijn, English his are all ambiguous). Entfesselt does not suppress the reflexive Possessive in Scandinavian Languages; it is a Feature to preserve, not to Entfesselt away. The Centroid recognizes it as the preferred possessive Construction when the Possessor is the Clause's Subject.
Rule 27: Separable Verb Prefix Normalization. German, Dutch, and Danish all have separable Verb Prefixes: the Verb consists of a base Verb and a Particle that separates from it under specific syntactic Conditions. German: aufmachen (auf + machen), anfangen (an + fangen), ausgehen (aus + gehen). Dutch: openmaken (open + maken), uitgaan (uit + gaan). Danish: at lukke op (lukke + op), at gå ud (gå + ud). The Particle Placement Rules differ across Languages, but the Phenomenon is shared and the spoken Tendency in all three is the same: the Particle follows the Verb in main Clauses. In German, prescriptive Grammar sends the Particle to clause-final Position in subordinate Clauses: weil ich die Tür aufgemacht habe. In spoken German, the Particle stays close to the Verb: weil ich hab die Tür aufgemacht. Entfesselt normalizes: in main Clauses, the Particle follows the Verb as in standard spoken Usage (ich mache die Tür auf). In subordinate Clauses, the Particle is not forced to clause-final Position; it stays with its Verb, following the Verb Position Relaxation of Rule 9. Attestation: separable Prefixes are Proto-Germanic Heritage, attested in Gothic (us-gaggan, "to go out"), Old English (ut-gangan), and Old Norse (ut-ganga). Cross-language Parallel: English phrasal Verbs (go out, come in, take off, give up) are the direct structural Equivalent. English never fuses the Particle onto the Verb in any Position; the Particle always remains a separate Word. Entfesselt moves the continental Languages toward this English-like Transparency.
Rule 28: Gender Simplification Pressure (Documentation Rule). This is a Documentation Rule, not an active Transformation. The Majority of Centroid Languages have independently converged on a common-neuter Gender System: Danish (en/et), Dutch (de/het), Swedish (en/ett), Norwegian (en/ei/et, effectively common+neuter in most Dialects), Old Frisian (collapsed early), English (lost entirely). German's three-Gender System (der/die/das) is the Outlier within the Family. This is consistent with Principle 8 (Majority Rule). Entfesselt does not force Gender Change on German. The three-Gender System is deeply embedded in German Morphology and cannot be stripped without restructuring the entire Determiner and Adjective Agreement System. However, the following is noted for the Record: (a) The Centroid Target for Gender is two Genders (common + neuter), per Principle 8. (b) German Dialects, particularly northern and some central Dialects, already show masculine-feminine Merger in informal Speech; the Merger is most advanced in Low German Varieties. (c) Yiddish, derived from the same MHG Stock, effectively reduced to two Genders in most Varieties. (d) Faroese and Icelandic Retention of three Genders alongside four Cases shows the System is viable when supported by full Case Morphology; German's Combination of three Genders with a weakening Case System is the unstable Configuration.
Rule 29: Infinitive Marker Placement. In German um...zu + Infinitive Constructions with separable-prefix Verbs, the Infinitive Marker zu is inserted between the Prefix and the Stem: anzufangen (an-zu-fangen), aufzumachen (auf-zu-machen), auszugehen (aus-zu-gehen). This splitting of the Infinitive Marker into the middle of the Verb is a Peculiarity of German prescriptive Orthography. Dutch te behaves identically: op te maken. Danish at behaves similarly with separable Constructions. Entfesselt normalizes: the Infinitive Marker precedes the full Verb including its Prefix, not inserted between Prefix and Stem. Anzufangen → zu anfangen. Aufzumachen → zu aufmachen. Auszugehen → zu ausgehen. The Prefix and Stem remain a Unit; the Infinitive Marker stands outside. This is a minor Regularization whose Value is structural Parallelism: the normalized Form makes the Construction visually identical across German, Dutch, and Danish, and parallel to English to go out (not to out-go or out-to-go). Attestation: MHG Infinitive Constructions did not consistently insert zu between Prefix and Stem; the Insertion was regularized during the prescriptive Standardization Period.
Rule 30: Adverbial Connector Correspondences (Documentation Rule). This is a Documentation Rule, not an active Transformation. Discourse Markers and adverbial Connectors serve identical Functions across the Centroid Languages. Each Language keeps its own Forms; the Parallel is noted for Learner Transparency and Centroid Dictionary Construction. Additive: German also/außerdem, Dutch ook/bovendien, Danish også/desuden, Swedish också/dessutom, Norwegian også/dessuten, English also/moreover. Contrastive: German trotzdem/dennoch, Dutch toch/desondanks, Danish dog/alligevel, Swedish ändå/dock, Norwegian likevel/dog, English nevertheless/still. Causal: German deshalb/deswegen, Dutch daarom/daardoor, Danish derfor, Swedish därför, Norwegian derfor, English therefore. Concessive: German obwohl/zwar, Dutch hoewel/weliswaar, Danish selvom/ganske vist, Swedish fastän/visserligen, Norwegian selv om, English although/admittedly. This Rule does not transform Text. It provides a Reference Table for the Centroid Dictionary Project and for Learners navigating between Centroid Languages.
Rule 31: Wer/Was as Indefinite/Free Relative Pronoun. German wer (who) and was (what) double as indefinite and free relative Pronouns: Wer das glaubt, ist dumm (whoever believes that is foolish), Was er sagt, stimmt (whatever he says is correct). This Usage is shared across the Centroid: Dutch wie dat gelooft, is dom; Danish hvem der tror det, er dum; Swedish vem som tror det är dum; Norwegian den som tror det, er dum; English whoever believes that is foolish. The Pattern is Proto-Germanic: the interrogative Pronoun serves double Duty as an indefinite/free Relative. Gothic hvas (who) and hva (what) function the same Way; Old English hwā and hwæt likewise. Prescriptive Grammars in German sometimes insist on more explicit Constructions: Derjenige, der das glaubt... or Jeder, der das glaubt... These are Latinate Expansions of a Construction that is already clear and concise. Entfesselt preserves the wer/was free relative Construction as the Default. It is native, attested in every Branch, and transparent across the Family.
Rule 32: Recipient Passive (bekommen/kriegen-passive). Standard German uses the agent-passive (werden-passive): Das Buch wurde ihm geschenkt (The Book was given to whom). The spoken Language universally prefers the Recipient Passive with bekommen or kriegen as the passive Auxiliary: Er bekommt das Buch geschenkt / Er kriegt das Buch geschenkt (he gets the Book given; the Book is given to whom). The grammatical Subject is the Recipient; the Construction foregrounds the Recipient's Experience rather than the Agent's Action or the Object's Displacement. Attested from early New High German (frühneuhochdeutsch). Universal across all German Dialects without Exception. Suppressed in formal Writing because Latin has no equivalent Construction — Latin's Passive is exclusively agent-passive — and the Prescriptivists treated any Construction without a Latin structural Parallel as "vulgar." Dutch has the identical Construction with krijgen: hij krijgt het boek cadeau gedaan. The English get-passive is the structural Parallel: he got given the Book, she got told the News, they got sent a Letter. Old English used weorþan (Cognate of German werden) as the primary passive Auxiliary but also allows a get-like Construction in early Texts. The Recipient Passive is native Germanic, structurally parallel across the Family, and suppressed exclusively by Latinate Prescriptivism. Entfesselt restores it as a fully valid passive Construction alongside the werden-passive. Both are correct; the Choice between them follows the same Logic as the Choice between agent-passive and recipient-passive in English.
Rule 33: Brauchen without zu. Standard German requires brauchen + zu + Infinitive in negative and limiting Contexts: Du brauchst nicht zu kommen (You don't need to come). The spoken Language universally drops zu: Du brauchst nicht kommen. This Form is stigmatized in formal Writing as a grammatical Error, but it is universal in Speech across all German Dialects, including formal spoken Registers. The underlying Process is Grammaticalization: brauchen is completing its Development from a full lexical Verb ("to have Need of") into a modal Auxiliary. All German modal Verbs (können, müssen, sollen, wollen, dürfen, mögen) take a bare Infinitive without zu. Brauchen is joining this Class in the spoken Language; the written Standard artificially arrests the Development midway. This is the same Pattern the Prescriptivists applied elsewhere: natural Germanic Grammaticalization allowed, then frozen at a prescribed Stage in the Name of a Latin-derived formal Rule. Parallel: Dutch hoeven (the functional Equivalent of brauchen in the "not need to" Construction) is fully grammaticalized as a Modal and takes a bare Infinitive: je hoeft niet te komen is the standard written Form, but je hoeft niet komen is already attested in informal Dutch. English fully completed the same Grammaticalization: modal need not takes a bare Infinitive — she need not come — while lexical need to takes a marked Infinitive — she needs to come. English distinguishes the two Stages cleanly; German is mid-transition. Entfesselt recognizes the bare-infinitive Form brauchst nicht kommen as valid and consistent with both the Centroid Direction and natural Germanic Grammaticalization in Progress.
Rule 34: Weil/Obwohl with V2 (Conjunction Reclassification). Rule 9 restores verb-second Word Order within subordinate Clauses introduced by weil and obwohl. This Rule documents a further Step that is happening in real Time: weil and obwohl are being reclassified from subordinating to coordinating Conjunctions by spoken German across all Dialects and Regions. The Surface Form is identical — Weil ich hab keine Zeit — but the grammatical Analysis is different. In Rule 9, weil remains a subordinating Conjunction and the finite Verb has moved leftward. In Rule 34, weil functions like denn: the Clause it introduces is syntactically a main Clause with ordinary V2 Order, not a subordinate Clause with a relocated Verb. The Distinction matters because the two Operations make different Predictions about Embedding, Scope, and Intonation. Attested in Writing from the late 18th Century; documented extensively by Variationists from the 1990s onward (Günthner 1993, Küper 1991). The Process is furthest advanced with weil, progressing with obwohl, and beginning with während and wenn in some Dialect Regions. Cross-language Parallel: English underwent the same Reclassification with because in informal Registers — I'm not going, because I don't want to — where because introduces what is effectively a free-standing Explanation rather than a subordinate Clause. The Intonation Break before because in such Sentences signals main-clause Status. The Germanic Tendency to reclassify explanatory Conjunctions from subordinating to coordinating is thus cross-family. Entfesselt treats V2 after weil/obwohl as unambiguously valid, consistent with both Rule 9 and this Reclassification Analysis. The spoken Language has already made this Choice; the written Standard's Insistence on verb-final is the Deviation.
Rule 35: Modal Particles as Legitimate Grammar (Documentation Rule). This is a Documentation Rule, not an active Transformation. German's modal Particles — doch, mal, ja, halt, eben, schon, wohl, eigentlich, bloß, nur, ruhig, vielleicht and others — are systematically excluded from formal Writing and stigmatized as "Filler," "Colloquialisms," or Marks of Imprecision. They are none of these. They are a grammaticalized System of evidential and Stance Markers: doch signals that the Speaker assumes shared Knowledge or expects Agreement; wohl marks inferential Evidentiality (the Speaker concludes rather than directly knows); ja marks established common Ground; halt/eben signal Resignation or Self-evidence; mal softens Imperatives and marks Tentativeness; schon signals Concession with Reservation. This is the Germanic Family's Answer to evidential Morphology — the grammatical Category (found in Turkish, Tibetan, and many other Languages) that marks how the Speaker knows what they claim to know. The Germanic Languages encode it as a separate Word Class rather than as verbal Morphology, but the Function is identical. Cross-family Parallels: Dutch toch/maar/wel/hoor/zeker; Danish jo/nok/vel/da/vist; Swedish ju/nog/väl/då/visst; Norwegian jo/nok/vel — exact functional Cognates across every Branch. Old Norse had equivalent Particles; the Eddic Texts use nú, þó, enn in Ways that parallel the modern Particle System. English has reduced the Inventory but not eliminated it: well, just, simply, really, I mean, you know carry similar stance-marking Functions in informal Speech, and the Category is recognizable. The Prescriptivists suppressed German modal Particles because they had no clean Latin Equivalents and because the 18th-century Ideal of Prose was impersonal and Latin-modeled. The Suppression is the same in Structure as every other Entfesselt Reversal: a native Germanic System delegitimized because it had no Latin Parallel. Entfesselt recognizes modal Particles as grammatically legitimate Elements of German, Dutch, and Scandinavian Prose. Their Absence in formal Writing is a prescriptive Artifact, not a Mark of Clarity.
Rule 36: Ethical/Free Dative. German Das ist mir ein guter Wein. Der ist mir zu teuer. Komm mir nicht zu spät nach Hause. The Dative of personal Involvement — expressing the Speaker's or a third Party's Stake in, Attitude toward, or affective Relationship with an Event — is universal in spoken German across all Dialects and Registers. It is systematically stripped from formal Prose as "colloquial" or "imprecise." Attestation: Gothic uses the Dative of Interest freely. Old Norse uses it standardly: þat er mér gott (that is good to/for me, with personal Involvement implicit). Old English: him þūhte (it seemed to him), hwæt mē Hrōþulf — the free Dative appears throughout the Corpus. English retains Traces: cry me a river, sing me a song, don't you dare talk to me like that, buy yourself something nice — these are not pure Datives of Purpose but carry the same personal-involvement Semantics. Dutch: dat is me wat, dat lijkt me wat, hij is me te oud — alive in all Registers. The Construction is Proto-Germanic, attested in all four Pillars (Gothic, Old Norse, Old English, Old Frisian), alive in every Dialect of German without Exception, and suppressed in formal Writing for the same Latinate Reason as every other Suppression: Latin's Dative of Interest exists but was stigmatized as a Colloquialism in prescriptive Latin Grammar, and that Stigma transferred to German. Entfesselt restores the free Dative as a fully valid Prose Element. It is not imprecise; it encodes something that formal German Prose cannot express without Circumlocution: the Speaker's personal affective Stake in the Proposition. Stripping it does not increase Clarity; it removes Information.
Rule 37: Present Tense for Future Reference (Documentation Rule). This is a Documentation Rule, not an active Transformation. German's werden-Future (Ich werde morgen kommen) is a relatively late Grammaticalization, not a Proto-Germanic Heritage Construction. It develops from a full lexical Verb meaning "to become" used in a prospective Sense, and its Grammaticalization into a pure future Auxiliary is still incomplete: werden in German retains modal Meaning (Probability, Expectation) alongside temporal Meaning, exactly as English will retains volitional Meaning alongside Futurity. Spoken German overwhelmingly uses the present Tense + Time Adverb for future Reference: Ich komme morgen. Er fährt nächste Woche. Wir sehen uns dann. The werden-Future is disproportionately formal and marks Distance, Formality, or modal Uncertainty rather than simple Futurity. Cross-family Attestation: Gothic uses the present Tense for future Reference as standard: þan qimiþ sunus mans (when the Son of Man comes — present for future). Old Norse: present for future is the Default in the Sagas. Old English: present for future is standard throughout the Corpus; the will/shall Auxiliaries were still fully modal in OE and only gradually grammaticalized into Futures. Dutch: present + Time Adverb is strongly preferred in spoken and informal written Dutch over the zullen-Future. Danish and Swedish: present for future is the normal spoken Form; the vil/ska-Future marks modal or emphatic Meaning, not default Futurity. English: I'm going tomorrow, the train leaves at six, she starts Monday — present for future is fully grammaticalized and unmarked. The werden-Future in formal German Writing is thus a Prestige Construction that pushes against the Grain of both the Germanic Family and German's own spoken Usage. It is not wrong; it is a valid Grammaticalization. But treating the present-tense Future as inferior or "imprecise" is the prescriptive Intervention. Entfesselt recognizes the present-for-future as the default Germanic Form and the analytically expected Choice. The werden-Future should be reserved for its residual modal Meaning (Probability, Expectation, polite Future) rather than imposed as the only correct future Reference in formal Prose.
Rule 38: z → ts (Consonant Shift Orthographic Transparency). German "z" is always pronounced /ts/ — an Affricate that is the direct Product of the High German Consonant Shift (t → ts in initial Position and after Consonants; t → z or tz in final Position after short Vowels; documented in Section 1.8). Standard German writes this Sound as "z" word-initially and word-finally, and as "tz" after short Vowels. English Speakers reading "z" expect the Fricative /z/ as in "zoo"; the Mismatch simultaneously misleads about Pronunciation and conceals the Cognate Relationship with English "t" Words that the Shift produced. Entfesselt German writes /ts/ uniformly as "ts", replacing both "z" and "tz" throughout: Word-initial z → ts: zehn → tsen (English: ten) · zu → tsu (English: to) · zwei → tswai (English: two) · Zeit → Tsait (English: tide, from PGmc *tīdiz) · Zahn → Tsan (English: tooth, from PGmc *tanþaz) · Zunge → Tsunge (English: tongue) · Zimmer → Tsimmer (English: timber) Medial and final z → ts: Herz → Herts (English: heart) · Schmerz → Schmerts (English: smart, from OE smeortan) · Salz → Salts (English: salt) · Witz → Wits (English: wit) · kurz → kurts tz → ts: Katze → Katse (English: cat) · sitzen → sitsen (English: sit) · jetzt → jetst (English: yet, cf. OE gīet) · Netz → Nets (English: net) · Hitze → Hits·e (English: heat) The Change does not touch "ss" or "ß": these represent the same Shift's medial and final Environment (t → ss/ß between and after Vowels — English water/eat/out = German Wasser/essen/aus) and are already spelled with Letters English Readers parse as /s/. The orthographic Problem is "z" specifically, which misleads; "ss" and "ß" do not. Scope: German and Yiddish (which shares the High German Consonant Shift and has the same /ts/ Value in the relevant Position, though Yiddish uses different Conventions in its Hebrew-alphabet Spelling). Not applicable to Dutch (where "z" represents /z/, the unshifted Stop; Dutch did not undergo the HG Shift). Low German and Plautdietsch are exempt for the opposite Reason: they did not undergo the Shift and therefore write "t" where High German writes "z" — their Spelling already shows the English Cognate transparently.

Case Remnants: Re-Centering English Morphology. The Spelling Changes above address Orthography. English also retains morphological Case, not in its Nouns (which lost it) but intact in its Pronouns. The English Pronoun System has kept the Germanic Case Oppositions for a thousand Years: nominative I / he / she / we / they / who; oblique (accusative and dative merged) me / him / her / us / them / whom; genitive my / his / her / our / their / whose. This maps directly onto German ich/mich/mir/mein, er/ihn/ihm/sein, wer/wen/wem/wessen. English collapsed accusative and dative into one oblique Form (the same Merger happening informally in many German Dialects today) but kept the nominative/oblique/genitive three-way Split in every Pronoun. The Apostrophe-s Genitive (the King's Crown) is the old Germanic Genitive Marker, exactly equivalent to German des Königs Krone and Swedish kungens krona.
A Re-centered English would treat these Remnants as active Conventions rather than fading ones: "whom" used consistently where German would use accusative or dative; the 's Genitive used wherever the underlying Relationship is possessive rather than displaced by the Norman French "of" Construction. No new Morphology is required: only what is already there.
Gender: the Centroid is two Genders. Old English had three grammatical Genders (masculine/feminine/neuter), but they were notoriously misaligned with natural Gender: wīf (Woman) was neuter, mǣden (Maiden) was neuter, wīfmann (Woman/Person) was masculine. The Mismatch made the System brittle, and it collapsed during early Middle English, probably accelerated by Contact with Old Norse Speakers whose Gender Assignments for the same Words were often different from the Old English ones.
Old Frisian underwent the same Collapse. Danish went from three Genders to two: common (en) and neuter (et). Dutch arrived at exactly the same Endpoint: common (de) and neuter (het). Swedish and Norwegian simplified along similar Lines. These are the Branches geographically and genetically closest to English; every one of them independently arrived at the same two-way System.
German alone maintained the full three-Gender System in its written Standard, though even German Dialects show ongoing Merger of masculine and feminine in many Regions. The Centroid's Gender System is therefore not German's der/die/das. It is the common/neuter Binary that the Majority of the Centroid's living Members converge on: OE and OF collapsed to it, Danish has en/et, Dutch has de/het, Swedish and Norwegian have common + neuter Variants. When the Majority of the Centroid's Members agree on a grammatical Feature, that Feature is the Centroid Target. Common + neuter is the Majority Position by a wide Margin. Re-centering English toward the Gender Centroid means pointing toward that two-way Split. English already makes this Distinction in its Pronouns: he/she for Persons and animate Beings (common), it for Objects and Abstractions (neuter). The Re-centering recognizes that English maintained the Centroid's Gender Distinction in the Pronoun System all along, not in the Article (which became a uniform the) but in the Pronoun, where it has never left.
On Noun Case Restoration. Case is a harder Question than Gender. English lost Case from Nouns entirely by the 14th Century; only the Pronouns kept it. Restoring Case Marking to Nouns would require choosing Declension Classes, deciding which of the four Cases to restore, and the Result would read as constructed rather than archaic. The preferable middle Ground: the genitive Apostrophe-s (the king's crown) is the Germanic Genitive already fully alive in English, and it should be used systematically over the Norman French of Construction wherever the underlying Relationship is possessive. The oblique Pronouns (me, him, her, us, them, whom) are already there and should be used consistently. The Re-centering amplifies what survived; it does not reconstruct what was lost.
If three-Gender Assignment is ever required (for Translation from German, or in literary Contexts where Agreement Marking matters), Gender Authority falls in strict Order of historical Depth:
1. Old English: the native English Gender, where attested.
2. Old Frisian: the closest continental Relative, where OE is unclear or lost.
3. Old Norse: the Scandinavian Anchor that shaped medieval English, where OE and OF are both unclear.
4. Modern German: last Resort; German Gender Assignments may reflect Latinate editorial Choices that OE and ON predate.
Since Old Frisian and Old Norse themselves collapsed to two Genders, the three-Gender Path will rarely be needed. The common/neuter Path is the one the Centroid took, and it is the one Re-centered English follows.
Afrikaans: the other Outlier. No Articles are gendered (die serves all), no Pronouns distinguish nominative from oblique. For the Centroid Project, Afrikaans is invaluable as Proof that a Germanic Language without prescriptive morphological Complexity is still fully recognizable as Germanic, validating the Thesis from the opposite Side. 5. Conclusion

The Germanic Languages are not distant Relatives. They are Siblings separated by Surface Conventions. The Conventions were imposed during a narrow historical Window (rouhly 1650–1850), by Prescriptivists in each Country who took Latin and French as their Models for what a serious written Language should look like. The Result was a Set of written Standards that systematically suppressed the Features the Germanic Languages share and strengthened the Features that distinguish whom. German got recursive Compound Stacking, mandatory verb-final Subordination, and synthetic Case Morphology codified beyond what any spoken Dialect actually uses. Danish absorbed Low German and High German compounding Habits through Hanseatic Trade. The Impositions varied; the Pattern was the same.

German with its Compounds split and its Word Order relaxed reads like English. English with its silent Consonants rationalized reads like Dutch.
