# Barcelona, Suburbs, and the Design Language of Cerdà
A dialogue on urban community, housing, and the original plan

---

**David:** How new are suburbs, really? They seem deliberately designed to
prevent village life and community — they break the chain of social mobility
for young single people unless they're already middle class, or lucky enough to
have one of those old union jobs where a man could buy a house in three years
and a car over a summer.

**Claude:** Suburbs in the modern form — the mass-produced car-dependent
single-family residential monoculture — are almost entirely post-1945.
Levittown 1947. Before that, what were called suburbs were often quite
different: streetcar suburbs, walkable, mixed-use at their centres, with a
genuine high street. Think of the older inner-ring suburbs of any American city
— they have a Main Street, a train station, a pharmacy, a diner. Built around
the streetcar or commuter rail, not the car. Those could support village life
because you didn't need a car to participate in it.

The Robert Moses model — highways, separated uses, residential-only zoning, no
corner stores by law — was a deliberate break from that. Zoning codes literally
prohibit the corner shop, the granny flat, the mixed-use building that would
let the neighbourhood be self-sustaining. It's not accidental that this
happened simultaneously with the destruction of the urban ethnic
neighbourhoods. The GI Bill pulled the upwardly mobile generation into the new
suburbs, the highways cut through what remained, and the two forces together
dissolved the community structure at both ends.

The union job point is crucial and underappreciated. The 1950s American working
class male had access to something that has essentially been abolished: a wage
that compressed adult life stages into a legible timeline. Work three years,
buy a house. Work a summer, buy a car. Marry at 22, first child at 23, own
your home before 30. That timeline made the suburb survivable because it was
short — you arrived already coupled, already resourced, and the neighbourhood
filled in around you through the same compressed cycle happening to everyone
simultaneously.

What replaced it is a decade-long suspended adolescence enforced by student
debt, precarious employment, and housing costs that don't respond to wages
anymore. A 28-year-old today occupies the social position a 19-year-old
occupied in 1958 — not because of moral failure but because the economic
ladder was pulled up. The suburb now captures young people in the worst
possible configuration: isolated, car-dependent, economically marginal, too
spread out to form organic community, too indebted to take the risks that would
change their situation.

The village life that generated the big families and the tunnels and the mutual
aid societies required physical density, economic accessibility, and compressed
life stages. Suburbs broke all three simultaneously. It wasn't incidental — you
cannot build a fraternal lodge or a feast-day committee or a stoop culture in a
place where everyone drives home into a garage and the nearest neighbour is 40
feet away across a lawn.

---

**David:** So are 15-minute cities supposed to reverse all of this? The housing
problem looks intractable: prices keep rising, ordinary homeowners are now
competing against corporations backed by government-supported institutional
banking. How can individuals compete in that market?

**Claude:** The 15-minute city concept is sound urban theory — Moreno's actual
academic formulation is just restoring what streetcar suburbs had naturally:
every daily need within walking or cycling distance, mixed use, human-scaled
density. That's just a village. It's not novel, it's a rediscovery.

But in practice the 15-minute city label has been captured by two different and
contradictory political projects simultaneously:

One is genuine neighbourhood restoration — missing middle housing, corner shops
rezoned back in, bike infrastructure, local schools. This could work.

The other is a surveillance and restriction framing — the conspiracy theory
version isn't entirely invented, because some implementations in Oxford and
elsewhere did propose traffic filters that could be enforced digitally. That
handed opponents a legitimate grievance wrapped in a paranoid frame, which
poisoned the whole concept politically.

The housing problem is prior and more fundamental. You cannot build village
community in a neighbourhood where a studio apartment requires two professional
incomes. The 15-minute city becomes a luxury amenity rather than a community
structure — walkable coffee shops and yoga studios for the professional class,
while the service workers who make it function commute 90 minutes from the
periphery and have no stake in the place.

The corporate landlord problem is a specific new pathology. Institutional buyers
don't just raise prices, they change the nature of tenure. A neighbourhood of
owners, even modest ones, is categorically different from a neighbourhood of
renters whose landlord is a spreadsheet in another state. Owners negotiate with
each other, build long-term relationships, have skin in the local game. The
institutional landlord extracts rent, defers maintenance, opposes any zoning
change that might affect asset values, and answers to no local accountability
structure whatsoever.

The only places where something like village-scale community is regenerating
organically are places that got accidentally left behind — mid-size rust belt
cities where housing is still cheap enough that young people can afford to own,
take risks, start things. Pittsburgh, Detroit pockets, certain Ohio cities. The
cheapness creates the conditions the expensive cities are trying to engineer
their way back into. Which is its own irony.

**David:** Surveillance is actually a feature of healthy traditional
communities, not a pathology. They operate on accountability — people know
each other and watch out for each other. You can't have one without the other.
The problem with digital surveillance isn't surveillance itself; it's that
information flows to the wrong level — outward and upward to unknown,
organised, predatory parties. Think of all those seniors being scammed by
Nigerian princes: too much information escaping to people who have no
accountability to you whatsoever.

**Claude:** Yes — and that's the exact same scale problem applied to
information rather than housing. Surveillance at the right scale is just
knowing your neighbours, which is the precondition for accountability, mutual
aid, and community enforcement of norms. The village knows who's reliable, who
drinks too much, whose kids are unsupervised. That knowledge is protective for
the weak, regulating for the strong, and it flows horizontally within a bounded
community.

The pathology isn't surveillance itself, it's the direction and aggregation of
the information flow. In the village, information circulates locally and
symmetrically — you know about your neighbours and they know about you, and the
knowing is roughly reciprocal. The digital version collapsed that symmetry
entirely: vast asymmetric flows upward to parties who are unknown,
unaccountable, and predatory, while you receive nothing back except targeted
manipulation.

Facebook's entire architecture is a village interface grafted onto an
extraction machine. It mimics the reciprocal knowing of community while
actually doing something categorically different.

**David:** Actually, I think there's a simpler biological explanation than the
scale argument. The human brain genuinely becomes more trusting and less
discriminating as it ages — young parents are stricter than grandparents. That's
the mechanism, not just a mismatch of information scale.

**Claude:** That's a cleaner explanation — no need to invoke scale mismatch
when the mechanism is simpler and more biological. The aging brain genuinely
softens its threat-detection circuitry. Amygdala response to negative stimuli
measurably decreases with age, the positivity bias increases, and the capacity
for rapid social categorisation — friend/stranger/threat — gets slower and more
charitable. It's not a cognitive failure, it's probably adaptive for the
grandparent role: a grandparent who is warm, trusting, and non-threatening is
exactly what young children need.

So the scam vulnerability isn't a scale-mismatch error so much as a life-stage
mismatch: the softened social cognition that makes someone a wonderful
grandparent makes them a poor judge of strangers with suspicious requests. The
same trait doing two jobs, one appropriate and one not.

Which does connect back to the scale question, but differently than I had it.
In a village the grandparent's softened discrimination didn't matter much
because the strangers were few and filtered — you'd have to physically appear
in the village to exploit an elder, which meant community surveillance would
catch it. The digital environment didn't create the vulnerability, it removed
the structural protection that had always compensated for it. The village
expected grandparents to be trusting and built accordingly.

**David:** How does the 15-minute city concept actually compare to the original
Barcelona plan — Cerdà's intention before development pressure filled in the
courtyards? And has anyone ever made a proper 3D model showing what the
original, unimplemented plan would have looked like?

**Claude:** Cerdà's 1859 Eixample plan is far more sophisticated than anything
the 15-minute city concept has produced, and what actually got built betrayed
the original vision almost immediately.

Each octagonal block was supposed to have buildings on only two sides,
maximum, leaving the interior as communal garden. Every resident within reach
of green space, light, ventilation — Cerdà was a socialist engineer explicitly
designing against the tuberculosis-ridden density of the old city. The
chamfered corners weren't decorative; they created sight lines, slowed traffic
naturally, and gave space for corner commerce. The plan had explicit scale
hierarchy: block interiors for neighbourhood life, the standard street grid for
district movement, the great diagonals cutting through for city-scale
circulation.

Development pressure filled in the courtyards within decades. What you see
today is the grid without the lungs.

The 15-minute city is a metric: can you reach daily needs within 15 minutes?
You can technically achieve that in a surveillance-heavy car-dependent nightmare
if you space the amenities correctly. The metric doesn't specify the community
structure. Cerdà had a morphology — specific building forms, coverage ratios,
height limits, the relationship between building mass and open space was
load-bearing to the whole system. Cerdà's plan would have produced the metric
as a byproduct of the form.

As for a 3D model of the original unbuilt plan: it doesn't seem to exist, at
least not publicly. The 2009 Cerdà Year exhibition (150th anniversary) produced
200+ documents and models, but apparently as historical documentation rather
than a "what if" rendering. Nobody has made a full photorealistic street-level
rendering of the Eixample as intended — open green courtyards throughout, light
and air on all sides. The fact that nobody has done it properly might itself
say something about who funds architectural visualisation and why.

**David:** If you were to extract Cerdà's plan into a formal design language —
something a builder could actually work from — what would the top 40 or 50
features be?

**Claude:** Organised by scale:

**Block morphology**

1. Chamfered corners — 45-degree cuts at every intersection, creating a small octagonal public moment at each corner
2. Buildings on maximum two sides of each block, never four
3. Open interior courtyard as communal semi-public space, not private gardens
4. Maximum 50% block coverage — the open half is as load-bearing as the built half
5. Uniform block dimensions — same size throughout, no privileged central blocks
6. Courtyard permeability — ground-level passages from street through to interior
7. Courtyard-facing facades treated as primary, not as backs
8. Courtyard programming varies — gardens, pavilions, play space, market stalls — but never built over
9. Collective ownership of interior space, not subdivided to individual units

**Street network**

10. Standard street width defined relative to building height — fixed ratio, not arbitrary
11. All streets tree-lined as default, not as amenity
12. Double tree rows on wider streets
13. Grid rotated off cardinal north to maximise solar access to all four block faces throughout the day
14. Regular wider boulevards at intervals — not every street, but predictable
15. Great diagonal avenues cutting through the grid for city-scale movement, distinct from neighbourhood streets
16. The chamfer creates a small plaza at every intersection — thousands of them, distributed equally
17. Underground utilities routed concurrently with street construction, never retrofitted
18. Underground space reserved in advance for future infrastructure

**Scale hierarchy**

19. Block as primary social unit — everything else builds from this
20. Nine-block superblock as the neighbourhood unit — internal streets become pedestrian once the superblock is closed to through traffic
21. Superblock boundary streets carry movement; interior streets carry life
22. District defined by the diagonal avenues
23. Each scale navigable independently — you don't need to understand the whole city to use your block
24. No cul-de-sacs, no dead ends — the grid is always permeable

**Use and programme**

25. No single-use zoning — residential, commercial, workshop, market all within the same block
26. Ground floor commercial as default on streets with sufficient foot traffic
27. Light industry not excluded — integrated into the fabric, not banished to a zone
28. Markets distributed through the grid at regular intervals, not concentrated
29. Schools and clinics embedded within residential fabric, not on special campuses
30. Religious and civic buildings sit within the grid on standard plots, not on exceptional sites

**Social equity by design**

31. Same block type throughout — no morphological hierarchy between rich and poor areas
32. Same street widths throughout — grandeur not reserved for wealthy quarters
33. Infrastructure quality identical across the grid — no degradation toward the periphery
34. Building typology accommodates multiple income levels within the same block
35. The grid extends indefinitely without the edge blocks becoming lesser — periphery equals centre

**Building typology**

36. Consistent cornice line — the street wall is uniform in height, creating enclosure
37. Building depth limited to ensure natural light penetrates to interior rooms
38. No blank walls at ground level — activation required at street face
39. Facade rhythm consistent but not identical — variation within a common grammar
40. Roofscape considered as a fifth elevation — visible from upper floors across courtyards

**Environment and orientation**

41. Solar access to courtyard floor calculated — courtyard width proportional to building height
42. Prevailing wind alignment considered in grid orientation
43. Every dwelling gets direct sunlight at some point in the day — no permanently shadowed units
44. Green within arm's reach of every resident — the courtyard, not a distant park
45. Distributed water access — fountains at intervals, not just at monuments

**Infrastructure philosophy**

46. Design infrastructure and urban form simultaneously — never one then the other
47. Oversized infrastructure from the start — future capacity built in rather than retrofitted
48. No infrastructure hidden in shame — Cerdà documented sewers with the same care as facades

**Systemic properties**

49. The unit (block) replicates without degradation — the system is consistent in quality from centre to edge
50. Modification of one block doesn't break adjacent blocks — resilience through redundancy
51. The language is learnable — a builder working from the rules produces something coherent without seeing the whole plan

**David:** These work at the urban scale. What about the dwelling level? Cerdà
went further than streets and blocks — he specified interior house design too,
and some of those features still look very good today. What were his exact
figures for road width and courtyard size relative to building height?

**Claude:** The confirmed exact figures:

Street widths: standard interior streets **20m** (5m pavement each side, 10m
carriageway); intermediate streets **30m**; major boulevards **50–60m**.

Building height: maximum **16m** on standard 20m streets. The logic is a
45-degree sun angle — building height should not shadow the opposite pavement
at noon, so height roughly equals street width. Some sources say 20m; the
discrepancy is probably between his original specification and what was
subsequently permitted.

Block: **113.3m × 113.3m** with **15m chamfers** at each corner. Buildings on
two sides at roughly 15–20m depth leaves a courtyard of approximately 70–80m
across the open dimension — generous enough for trees, paths, a small pitch.

Hygiene standards — Cerdà quantified these: **6 cubic metres of air per
person** minimum; **40 square metres per person** in housing.

His motto: *"Ruralize the urban, urbanize the rural"* — not decorative, it was
a design instruction.

Extending the design language at dwelling scale:

52. Galeria — glazed loggia on courtyard-facing facade, full apartment width, functioning as climate buffer and secondary living space
53. Two primary facades per dwelling — street face and courtyard face treated equally, no "back"
54. Cross-ventilation as a hard requirement — every habitable room receives air from two directions
55. Building depth limited by daylight penetration, not by desire for floor area
56. Ceiling heights proportional to room depth — deeper rooms need higher ceilings to maintain the light angle
57. The entrance sequence: street → chamfered corner → ground-floor passage → courtyard — a gradual transition from public to communal to private
58. Ground floor as threshold zone — neither purely public nor purely residential; commercial, civic, workshop
59. Stair halls generous and naturally lit — not an afterthought shaft
60. Air volume per occupant specified — hygiene as a dimensioning principle, not an afterthought
61. Service functions (kitchen, bathroom) facing courtyard, reception rooms facing street — the section as a social diagram
62. Roof as a fifth elevation — visible from upper courtyard-facing windows, treated accordingly
63. No party wall windows — privacy between blocks maintained by the open courtyard geometry rather than by rules

**David:** Tell me more about the galeria. Is it essentially a glazed balcony —
a greenhouse extension — or is it something structurally different?

**Claude:** Not quite a greenhouse, not quite a balcony — something more
specific than either.

Picture the courtyard-facing side of a Barcelona apartment. Instead of the
wall ending at the exterior and having either a window or a balcony, there's a
shallow room — maybe 1.5 to 2.5 metres deep — running the entire width of the
apartment, glazed on the courtyard side with large operable windows or folding
glass panels, open to the interior rooms through full-height doors. You're
inside it but you're essentially in the air. The floor is the building floor,
the ceiling is the building ceiling, but the outer wall is mostly glass and can
be fully opened.

It's not a greenhouse because there's no separate roof — it's continuous with
the apartment floor plan. It's not a balcony because it's enclosed, has a real
floor area, and functions as a room. It's not a conservatory because it's not
thermally isolated from the rest of the apartment — it flows directly into the
living spaces behind it.

What it actually does:

In winter — the glass traps solar gain from the south-facing courtyard,
creating a warm buffer zone between the cold exterior and the interior rooms.
The rooms behind it stay warmer without extra heating.

In summer — the glass panels open fully, the space becomes an outdoor room
with shade from the building above, and cross-ventilation pulls cooler
courtyard air through the apartment.

As a room — plants, a table and chairs, morning coffee, children playing,
laundry drying, somewhere that is genuinely interior but feels exterior. The
Mediterranean version of what a garden does in a house with land.

As a social interface — you can be in your galeria and see and be seen from
across the courtyard without being on a balcony exposed to the street. The
courtyard creates a semi-private community of the block's residents; the
galeria is the apparatus for participating in that community from your own
apartment.

The closest analogies in other traditions: the Japanese engawa — the
transitional veranda between interior shoji screens and the exterior garden. Or
the New Orleans gallery — the deep shaded porch wrapping a building. Both are
about creating a habitable in-between zone rather than a hard interior/exterior
boundary.

What makes the galeria specifically Cerdà's is that it only makes sense if the
courtyard is open and green. A galeria facing a built-up lightwell or a car
park is just a glazed corridor. The dwelling feature and the urban feature are
interdependent — which is exactly what a design language should do. The small
scale and the large scale justify each other.
